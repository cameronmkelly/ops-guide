Proofing Concepts

Scientific principles governing spirit dilution: louching, saponification, and ABV risk zones.

This document explains the chemistry behind reducing spirit proof with water — not just the steps, but why those steps matter. Understanding these principles helps operators make better real-time decisions when problems arise during proofing.

Louching (Chill Haze / Flocking)

Louching occurs when certain compounds that were soluble in high-alcohol solution precipitate out as water concentration increases, causing the spirit to turn cloudy or hazy. Louching may be reversible by increasing temperature.

Common contributing compounds include grain oils, botanical oils with long carbon chains (12–18 carbons), and fatty acids originating from lipid breakdown during fermentation. All of these are soluble at higher ABV but fall out of solution as the water-to-alcohol ratio shifts.

Saponification

Saponification occurs when minerals or salts in the dilution water react with fatty acids in the spirit, forming insoluble compounds that create cloudiness or sediment. Unlike louching, saponification is typically irreversible.

This is why the Proofing SOP requires exclusive use of RO Water. City water and well water carry minerals that will react with the spirit. The RO system removes these minerals before the water is used for proofing.

ABV Risk Zones

Low Risk
95% – 57% ABVLarge reduction steps (10–20%) are safe. Compounds remain highly soluble at these ABV levels.
Moderate
57% – 52% ABVReduce step size to 3–5% per addition. Monitor closely for early signs of haze.
High Risk
52% – 45% ABVCritical zone. Reduce to 2–3% per step. Many long-chain compounds reach their precipitation threshold in this range.
Moderate
44% – 40% ABVRisk decreases below 45%. Steps of 2–5% are generally safe to bottling proof.
